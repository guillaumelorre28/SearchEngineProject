
The Berlin film festival <https://www.theguardian.com/film/berlinfilmfestival> 
is to stage tributes to Alan Rickman and David Bowie, it has been announced.

In a wide-ranging press conference, festival director Dieter Kösslick provided 
more details of the festival’s programme, alongside already revealed events 
such as theworld premiere of Terence Davies’ A Quiet Passion 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2016/jan/18/terence-davies-emily-dickinson-a-quiet-passion-premiere-berlin-film-festival-cynthia-nixon>
 and anopening gala presentation of the Coen brothers’ Hail, Caesar! 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2015/dec/04/coen-brothers-hail-caesar-to-open-berlin-film-festival>

The festival, which opens on 11 February, will screen Ang Lee’s Sense and 
Sensibility 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2011/dec/26/favourite-film-sense-and-sensibility>
, in which Rickman plays the key supporting role of Colonel Brandon, while the 
Bowie tribute will be the 1976 Nicolas Roeg filmThe Man Who Fell to Earth 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2012/jun/14/man-who-fell-to-earth-review>.

The competition for the Golden Bear has been finalised and will include Thomas 
Vinterberg’sThe Commune <http://www.imdb.com/title/tt3082854/?ref_=nm_flmg_dr_2>
, inspired by his own childhood; Alex Gibney’s documentary Zero Days, about 
cybercrime and internet surveillance;Genius 
<http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1703957/>, directed by Michael Grandage; and Mia 
Hansen-Løve’sThings to Come 
<http://www.imdb.com/title/tt4120176/?ref_=nm_flmg_dr_1>.

Also screening in competition will be Alone in Berlin 
<http://www.imdb.com/title/tt3026488/>, an adaptation of the celebrated 
Nazi-resistance novel, andMidnight Special 
<http://www.imdb.com/title/tt2649554/>, from Mud director Jeff Nichols, about a 
man trying to protect his gifted son from government agents.

Joining the Coens and Terence Davies in the festival’s non-competitive 
sections will be Spike Lee, with his anti-gun parable Chi-Raq, Don Cheadle with 
his Miles Davis biopicMiles Ahead 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2015/oct/10/miles-ahead-review-don-cheadle-miles-davis-biopic>
, and Michael Moore with his satirical documentaryWhere to Invade Next 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2015/sep/11/where-to-invade-next-film-review-michael-moore-happy-toronto-tiff>
. Also showing will be experimental documentaryThe Seasons in Quincy: Four 
Portraits of John Berger <http://www.imdb.com/title/tt5298558/>, made by four 
directors including Tilda Swinton.

The increasing status of TV work is also reflected in the festival’s 
programme. Berlin will show episodes from series two ofBetter Call Saul 
<https://www.theguardian.com/tv-and-radio/better-call-saul> as well as two BBC 
miniseries:Love, Nina 
<https://www.theguardian.com/media/2015/feb/16/nick-hornby-writes-first-tv-drama-love-nina>
, adapted from the book by Nina Stibbe about working for literary editor 
Mary-Kay Wilmers, and The Night Manager, starringTom Hiddleston 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2016/jan/24/tom-hiddleston-i-am-fascinated-by-private-vulnerability-the-night-manager>
.

The Berlin film festival <https://www.berlinale.de/en/HomePage.html> runs from 
11 to 21 February.
