
Turkish prosecutors have issued arrest warrants for 243 members of the 
military in 54 provinces across the country, in an investigation related to 
July’s failed coup <https://www.theguardian.com/world/turkey-coup-attempt>, the 
state-run Anadolu news agency said.

 Anadolu said on Wednesday the suspects were being sought on suspicion they 
were using Bylock, an encrypted smartphone messaging app the government says 
was used by the network of Fethullah Gülen, the US-based cleric accused by 
Ankara of orchestrating the attempted coup.

The arrest warrants are the latest signs of the Turkish government’s attempts 
to exert more control over the country.

According to Anadolu, more than 37,000 people have been formally arrested 
since the coup. About 100,000 more civil servants were fired or suspended 
during recent purges. Academics and teachers have lost their jobs andleading 
media outlets have been closed down 
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/2016/oct/30/turkey-shuts-media-outlets-terrorist-links-civil-servants-press-freedom>
.

President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan 
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/recep-tayyip-erdogan> is seeking to pass a 
bill 
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/2017/jan/16/bill-to-cement-erdogans-power-passes-first-vote-in-turkey>
that creates an executive presidency and abolishes an independent judiciary.
