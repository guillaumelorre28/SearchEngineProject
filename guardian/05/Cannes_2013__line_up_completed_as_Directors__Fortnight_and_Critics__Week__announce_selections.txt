
The line-up of this year's Cannes film festival 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/cannesfilmfestival> is now complete after the 
announcement of the Directors' Fortnight and Critics' Week selections.

The Director's Fortnight has added 20 titles to its already-announced opener, 
The Congress, from Ari "Waltz With Bashir 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/124299/waltz.with.bashir>" Folman, a 
part-animated adaptation of Stanislaw "Solaris 
<https://www.theguardian.com/books/data/book/science-fiction/9780571219728/solaris>
" Lem's sci-fi novelThe Futurological Congress 
<https://www.theguardian.com/books/data/book/science-fiction/9780749305291/the-futurological-congress>
.

Highlights include La Danza de la Realidad 
<http://www.imdb.com/title/tt2301592/>, the first film for more than two 
decades from cult Chilean director Alejandro Jodorowsky (best known forEl Topo 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/118037/el-topo>), and a complementary 
documentary,Jodorowsky's Dune <http://www.imdb.com/title/tt1935156/>, about the 
director's disastrous attempt to film Frank Herbert's giant novel. Two more 
Chilean directors, Sebastian Silva, with hisSundance hit Magic Magic 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2013/jan/25/magic-magic-first-look-review>, 
starring Michael Cera, and Marcela Said with The Summer of the Flying Fish, 
have had films selected alongside.

Directors Fortnight artistic director Edouard Waintrop has ensured a sizeable 
US presence: room has been found for Jim Mickle's cannibal thriller remake We 
Are What We Are with Bill Sage and Ambyr Childers, and Jeremy Saulnier's 
Kickstarter funded revenge thrillerBlue Ruin 
<http://www.kickstarter.com/projects/958674053/blue-ruin/>, described as about 
a "beach bum whose quiet life is upended by dreadful news".

After a shut-out in the main Festival competition, British representation 
comes by way of the second feature fromThe Arbor  
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/138408/arbor>director Clio Barnard 
<https://www.theguardian.com/culture/2010/sep/09/first-sight-clio-barnard>, 
whose The Selfish Giant is a contemporised adaptation of theOscar Wilde short 
story 
<http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Happy_Prince_and_Other_Tales#.22The_Selfish_Giant.22>
. Irish directorRuairi Robinson <http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruairi_Robinson> 
is there with his debut, Last Days of Mars, a Red Planet-set horror with Liev 
Schreiber and Romola Garai, while Gangs of Wasseypur's Anurag Kashyap has had 
his follow-up, Ugly, selected. Another notable returning absentee is Marcel 
"Sorrow and the Pity" Ophuls, who brings to Cannes his first full length film 
since 1994's Veillées d'Armes, an autobiographical piece called Un Voyageur.

The Critics Week has also unveiled its Cannes programme 
<http://www.semainedelacritique.com/EN/selection.php>, with the opening film 
slot going to Suzanne, from French director Katell Quillévéré, about a woman 
who abandoned her child as a teenager. AnotherSundance hit, Ain't Them Bodies 
Saints 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2013/jan/25/aint-them-bodies-saints-review>, 
makes its appearance here, as does another British effort, the Scotland-set For 
Those in Peril from writer-director Paul Wright.Eric Cantona 
<https://www.theguardian.com/sport/blog/2012/dec/09/eric-cantona-screen-break> 
will also put in an appearance, alongsode Beatrice Dalle and Alain-Fabien Delon 
in French sex comedy Les Rencontres d'Après Minuit. The Critics Week closing 
film is yet to be announced.

The 2013 Cannes film festival runs from 15 to 26 May.
