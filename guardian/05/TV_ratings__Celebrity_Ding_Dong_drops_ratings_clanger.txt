
Alan Carr <https://www.theguardian.com/culture/alan-carr>, who earlier this 
year signed a £3m two-year golden handcuff deal with Channel 4, returned for a 
new series of his panel show Celebrity Ding Dong with just 1.3 million viewers 
on Friday, October 10.

Alan Carr's Celebrity Ding Dong pulled in 7% of the audience between 10pm and 
10.50pm, narrowly beating Channel Five's Law & Order: Special Victims Unit, 
which drew 1.2 million.

But Carr could not beat a repeat of BBC2's Stephen Fry panel show QI, which 
picked up 2.7 million viewers and a 14% share.

QI also beat the third of the new series of ITV1's Al Murray's Happy Hour, 
which attracted 2.2 million viewers and a 12% share. The slot was won by the 
BBC News at 10pm on BBC1, with 4.6 million and 24%.

Another 90,000 viewers watched the new Carr show on digital catch-up service 
Channel 4 +1.

Landmark BBC2 documentary series The American Future: a History, by Simon 
Schama, opened up its four-part series with 2.3 million viewers and an 11% 
share at 9pm.

It beat the penultimate episode of Ugly Betty on Channel 4, which pulled in 
1.4 million viewers (and another 167,000 on Channel 4 +1), and another US 
drama, Five's NCIS, with 1.5 million.

ITV1's Wire in the Blood won the 9pm slot with 4.7 million viewers and a 22% 
share, beating the last in the six-part run of BBC1's Harry & Paul, which 
finished with 3.7 million viewers and 17%, and Little Britain USA, which drew 
4.2 million and 20%.

Earlier, the first of Five's new cop docusoap Police Interceptors averaged 1.7 
million viewers and a 7% share of the audience at 8pm.

Five's highest rating show of the night, Police Interceptors, beat Channel 4's 
A Place in the Sun: Home or Away, which averaged 1.1 million viewers and a 5% 
share, with another 154,000 viewers watching on Channel 4 +1.

Mastermind and Gardener's World, on at the same time on BBC2, averaged 2.1 
million and 2 million viewers respectively.
