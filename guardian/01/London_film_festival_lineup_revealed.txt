
Warring ballet divas, a reluctant king of England, and an aged Thai gent who 
can recall his past lives will be among the heroes and heroines of this year's
London film festival <http://www.bfi.org.uk/lff/>, the full lineup for which 
was announced earlier today.

Mark Romanek's Never Let Me Go 
<https://www.theguardian.com/books/2010/aug/06/kazuo-ishiguro-london-film-festival>
, an adaptation of the novel by Kazuo Ishiguro starring Keira Knightley, Carey 
Mulligan and Andrew Garfield, and Danny Boyle's latest venture,mountain 
climbing drama 127 Hours 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2010/aug/13/danny-boyle-127-hours-festival>, 
have already been announced as the films that will open and close the festival. 
The festival's gala section will also play host to a first UK showing for 
Venice hit Black Swan, Darren Aronofsky's psychological thriller starring 
Natalie Portman and Mila Kunis as rival dancers in a New York production of 
Swan Lake.

David Seidler's The King's Speech 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2009/nov/16/the-kings-speech-colin-firth>, 
which is also screening, centres on the struggles of King George VI (Colin 
Firth) to overcome a speaking impediment in order to address his new subjects 
with authority following the sudden abdication of his brother Edward VIII, in 
1936. Also based on a true story is Tony Goldwyn's Conviction, which stars 
Hilary Swank as an unemployed single mother who puts herself through law school 
at Roger Williams University. It is being tipped as the new Erin Brockovich.

A number of films which made their debut at Cannes will also screen, most 
notably the Palme d'Or winning film,Uncle Boonmee Who Can Recall His Past Lives 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/135882/uncle-bonmee-who-can-recall-his-past-lives>
. Thai director Apichatpong Weerasethakul's film focuses on an old man who 
explores his past lives as he contemplates the reasons for a fatal illness 
which is about to consume him. Mike Leigh'sAnother Year 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/135875/another-year>, described as "a 
bittersweet salute to the terrors and pleasures of growing old" 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2010/may/17/cannesfilmfestival-mikeleigh>by 
the Guardian's Xan Brooks, also screened at Cannes, though it failed to win any 
awards. It was, nevertheless, probably the festival's best-reviewed film.

The gala section's international flavour continues with Mexican film-maker 
Alejandro González Iñárritu'sBiutiful 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/135869/biutiful>, starring Javier 
Bardem. It is Iñárritu's first film in Spanish since his debut feature Amores 
Perros. Also intriguing is Julian Schnabel'sMiral 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/137727/miral>, a chronicle of Hind 
al-Husseini's effort to establish an orphanage in Jerusalem after the 1948 
partition of Palestine, which stars Slumdog Millionaire's Freida Pinto.

Guests who are expected to attend the festival in some capacity include 
Knightley, Mulligan, Julianne Moore, Swank, Helena Bonham Carter, Naomie 
Harris, Schnabel and Firth. Aside from the gala section the festival will play 
host to segments on new British cinema, French film-making, European film and 
cinema from around the world. There will also be a selection of movies from the 
past and an experimental section.

Organisers will once again celebrate the best films of the festival with an 
awards ceremony on 27 October. TheLondon film festival 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/londonfilmfestival> runs from 13 to 28 
October at venues across the capital.
