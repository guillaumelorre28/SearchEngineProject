
Monday

The 2016 Chichester season begins with Stiles and Drewe’s musical version of 
Graham Greene’sTravels with my Aunt at the Minerva <https://www.cft.org.uk/>. 
Paines Plough are atAssembly Roxy in Edinburgh 
<http://www.assemblyroxy.com/event.php?id=29> tonight, with Sabrina Mahfouz’s 
With a Little Bit of Luck 
<http://www.painesplough.com/current-programme/by-date/with-a-little-bit-of-luck>
, a play with music exploring the legacy of UK garage, touring this week in 
Sheffield, Lincoln, Bordon and Falmouth. Craig Taylor’s One Million Tiny Plays 
About Britain, taken fromhis Guardian column 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/series/one-million-tiny-plays-about-britain>
, is atthe Watermill near Newbury 
<https://www.watermill.org.uk/one_million_tiny_plays_about_britain>. Brad Birch 
captures the age of anxiety and paranoia and the stress of being a teacher in
The Brink 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2016/apr/12/the-brink-review-brad-birch-orange-tree>
 at theOrange Tree in Richmond <https://www.orangetreetheatre.co.uk/home>.

Tuesday

The high price of love is explored in The Ricochet Project’s brainy and 
beautifulSmoke and Mirrors, at Jackson’s Lane <http://www.jacksonslane.org.uk/>
, north London, as part ofCircusFest 2016 
<http://www.roundhouse.org.uk/whats-on/2016/circusfest-2016/>. Another 
Edinburgh hit, the quirkyLemons, Lemons, Lemons, Lemons, Lemons 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2015/aug/15/lemons-lemons-lemons-lemons-lemons-review-edinburgh-festival-southside-walrus-theatre>
 is atthe Greenbank in Bristol 
<https://www.tobaccofactorytheatres.com/shows/lemons-lemons-lemons-lemons-lemons/>
 this week, a clever little show about how saying less can be made to mean 
more. Filter’s Twelfth Night proves that music is the food of love at the
Nuffield in Southampton 
<http://www.nuffieldtheatre.co.uk/whats-on/twelfth-night>. Right Now is really 
unsettling – part thriller and part exploration of grief – and it’s at the
Traverse in Edinburgh 
<http://traverse.co.uk/whats-on/event-detail/766/right-now.aspx> from tonight. 
New Diorama director David Byrne’s version ofDown and Out in Paris and London 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2015/aug/06/edinburgh-festival-down-and-out-in-paris-and-london-review-orwell-meets-toynbee>
 by George Orwell was good in Edinburgh last year and should be even better 
when it arrives atLondon’s New Diorama 
<http://newdiorama.com/whats-on/down-out-in-paris-and-london>. Growing up with 
depression is the subject of Cheryl Martin’s Alaska, which is atthe Albany, 
Deptford <http://www.thealbany.org.uk/event_detail/1652/Theatre/Alaska->, 
tonight as part of A Nation’s Theatre. The audience decides what the future 
will be like in Remote, the latest from the always interesting Coney, atCamden 
People’s theatre <https://www.cptheatre.co.uk/production/remote/> from tonight.

Wednesday

Barely Methodical Troupe had a success with Bromance and they’re back with a 
new show, Kin, inspired by Lord of the Flies, which is atthe Roundhouse 
<http://www.roundhouse.org.uk/whats-on/2016/circusfest-2016/barely-methodical-troupe-kin-/>
 in London as part of CircusFest 16. Jack Dean’s Grandad and the Machine, a 
fairytale for grown-ups, is atARC in Stockton 
<http://arconline.co.uk/whats-on/drama-and-spoken-word/grandad-and-the-machine> 
tonight only. The friendships of a group of men living in a Nottingham hostel 
are explored in Daniel Hoffmann-Gill’s Kings, directed by Fiona Buffini at
Nottingham Playhouse 
<http://www.nottinghamplayhouse.co.uk/whats-on/drama/kings-2016/> from tonight. 
It’s your last chance atShoreditch Town Hall, London 
<http://www.thewardrobeensemble.com/>, for the Wardrobe Ensemble’s lovely 1972: 
The Future of Sex 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2015/aug/23/1972-the-future-of-sex-at-edinburgh-festival-review-high-price-of-free-love>
. In the main house atManchester’s Royal Exchange 
<http://www.royalexchange.co.uk/whats-on-and-tickets/king-lear> you can see Don 
Warrington in brilliant form inKing Lear 
<https://www.theguardian.com/stage/2016/apr/07/king-lear-review-royal-exchange-manchester>
, and in the studio you can catch Jon Spooner and Chris Thorpe inAm I Dead Yet? 
<http://www.royalexchange.co.uk/whats-on-and-tickets/am-i-dead-yet>, a 
cabaret-style show about dying. Chris Hannan’s new version ofThe Iliad takes to 
the Lyceum <http://lyceum.org.uk/whats-on/production/the-iliad> stage in 
Edinburgh. TheShakespeare in Shoreditch festival <http://www.r-ft.co.uk/sins/> 
begins with responses to Shakespeare’s plays from Lulu Raczka and others.

Thursday

The latest from Will Adamsdale is called The Joke and is about an Englishman, 
an Irishman and a Scotsman in a theatre, and it is on this week at theTheatre 
Royal in Margate <http://theatreroyalmargate.com/event/the-joke/>.

Friday and the weekend

Toneelgroep’s Kings of War, an epic of politics and war hewn from Shakespeare, 
is directed byIvo van Hove at the Barbican 
<http://www.barbican.org.uk/theatre/event-detail.asp?ID=17991>. Chris Goode & 
Company premiere new work as part of theTransform festival in Leeds 
<http://transformfestival.org/> this weekend. Howard Davies directs Hugh 
Bonneville in An Enemy of the People in preview atChichester Festival theatre 
<https://www.cft.org.uk/whats-on/event/an-enemy-of-the-people>. Improbable has 
got a new show and that’s always worth celebrating: Opening Skinner’s Box tries 
to make sense of who we are, and it’s atNorthern Stage in Newcastle 
<https://www.northernstage.co.uk/whats-on/opening-skinners-box> from tonight. 

Also tonight and tomorrow, Summerhall in Edinburgh hosts Other Faces 
<http://www.summerhall.co.uk/2016/other-faces/>, a mix of club night and 
experimental theatre. A good weekend atDerby theatre 
<https://www.derbytheatre.co.uk/learning-how-die> where tonight in the Studio 
Luca Rutherford’s Learning How to Die explores our inability to accept 
mortality, and tomorrow Sh!t Theatre entertain with theircabaret-style Women’s 
Hour <https://www.derbytheatre.co.uk/womens-hour>. A trip of a lifetime 
following a diagnosis of motor neurone disease is explored in Cell, by Smoking 
Apples and Dogfish, which is at theMumford theatre in Cambridge 
<https://tickets.mumfordtheatre.anglia.ac.uk/show.asp> on Saturday night as 
part of a longer tour. On Saturday, you can catch the fascinating The Money at 
theQuarterhouse in Folkestone 
<https://www.quarterhouse.co.uk/whats-on/the-money>. There’s a terrific lineup 
of writers – Caroline Bird, Lulu Raczka, Suhayla El-Bushra and Chris Thorpe – 
forThe Iphigenia Quartet starting at the Gate 
<http://www.gatetheatre.co.uk/events/all-productions/the_iphigenia_quartet> in 
Notting Hill on Saturday. If you are near Platform inEasterhouse, Glasgow 
<http://www.platform-online.co.uk/index.html?s=events&ec=viewEvent&event_id=749>
, on Saturday there is an equally commendable lineup in theOutskirts festival 
<http://www.platform-online.co.uk/index.html?s=events&ec=viewEvent&event_id=749>
 including one-on-one circus, Ishbel McFarlane’s O is for Hoolet, Company of 
Wolves, and much more. It’s all free too.

On Sunday, the Park theatre in Finsbury Park 
<https://www.parktheatre.co.uk/whats-on/hamlet-whos-there> plays host to Kelly 
Hunter’s Hamlet, Who’s There? which looks at Shakespeare’s play through the 
lens of grief.
