
Preparations are under way to mark the centenary of the Armenian genocide. 
According to historical estimates 
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/apr/12/pope-boosts-armenias-efforts-to-have-ottoman-killings-recognised-as-genocide>
 1.5 million people lost their lives in state-organised violence at the hands 
of Ottoman forces, in what was then eastern Turkey.

The massacre, which is commemorated on 24 April 2015, also saw hundreds and 
thousands of Armenians displaced 
<http://www.armenian-genocide.org/genocide.html>.

The term genocide is controversial. Turkey has refused to recognise the event, 
claiming tha 
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/2014/apr/24/armenian-president-turkey-genocide-denial>
t although between 300,000 – 500,000 Armenians died, at least as many Turks 
lost their lives as Armenians rose up against their Ottoman rulers siding with 
invading Russian troops.

In recent years the Turkish government has made moves to recognise the 
suffering, butArmenia <https://www.theguardian.com/world/armenia> has accused 
the Turks of continuing to ignore the facts.

In special mass at the Vatican on Sunday, Pope Francis called the massacre  
<https://www.theguardian.com/world/2015/apr/12/pope-boosts-armenias-efforts-to-have-ottoman-killings-recognised-as-genocide>
“the first genocide of the 20th century”, a message which Turkey received with 
“great disappointment and sadness”.
