
Religious belief was marginalised under the official state atheism of the 
Soviet Union and its satellites. However, from the 1960s onwards, some eastern 
bloc composers turned to spiritual themes. These were subversive acts, but, as 
Alex Ross explained inThe Rest is Noise 
<https://www.theguardian.com/books/2009/mar/14/rest-is-noise-alex-ross>, "the 
long twilight of the Brezhnev regime brought a midnight harvest of religious 
music."

The Estonian composer Arvo Pärt's Cantus in memorium Benjamin Britten appeared 
in 1977, and in 1982 was described by the Guardian's Meirion Bowen as a"Soviet 
equivalent to the minimalist style."

Fifteen years after its 1976 composition, Henryk Górecki's Symphony No.3 was 
recorded with soprano Dawn Upshaw 
<http://static.guim.co.uk/sys-images/Guardian/Pix/pictures/2013/11/4/1383572653939/part-001.jpg>
, and became a chart-topping best-seller. The music was dubbed "holy 
minimalism". The Polish composer gave his first interview in 20 years to the 
Observer in 1989.
 The Observer, 2 April 1989. Click on image to read full article. 
Sofia Gubaidulina 
<https://www.theguardian.com/music/2013/oct/31/sofia-gubaidulina-unchained-melodies>
's Offertorium, completed in 1980, quotes Bach's Musical Offering, but reflects 
the composer's own Christian faith. As such, it was not approved of in the 
pre-Glasnost Soviet Union. Meirion Bowen interviewed her in 1988.

Cornelius Cardew formed The Scratch Orchestra in London in the early 1970s. 
 The Guardian, 26 January 1970. Click on image. 
It was created from his musical class at Morley College, London, the idea 
being that anyone could join. As such, graphical scores were used rather than 
traditional sheet music.
