
The 66th edition of the Edinburgh film festival wrapped up on the weekend with 
a high-profile screening of Pixar's Scots-themed animationBrave 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/146821/brave>, and took the opportunity 
todish out a handful of awards 
<http://www.edfilmfest.org.uk/news/2012/06/award-winners-at-eiff-2012> – a 
practice that had beenabandoned for last year's controversy-mired event 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/2011/jun/20/edinburgh-film-festival-what-went-wrong>
.

The Michael Powell award for best British feature film went to One Mile Away 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/147744/one-mile-away>, Penny Woolcock's 
documentary about the attempt to forge a truce between two London gangs. This 
follows the Sheffield Doc/Fest's bestowal of its Inspiration award on Woolcock, 
best known for the opera film The Death of Klinghoffer and the urban musical1 
Day <https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/132456/1-day>.

Winner of the award for best film in the international feature competition was 
Here, Then <https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/148188/here-then>, a study 
of alienated youth in contemporary China from director Mao Mao, while special 
mention was given to documentaryPapirosen 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/148153/papirosen>, Gastón Solnicki's 
portrait of his own Argentinian-Jewish family, which the jury commended for its 
"beauty, pain and urgency".

Edinburgh's main awards were rounded out by best performance in a British 
feature film, which was given jointly to Bríd Brennan and Andrea Riseborough, 
who play mother and daughter in Irish-terrorism thrillerShadow Dancer 
<https://www.theguardian.com/film/movie/145818/shadow-dancer>, directed by 
James Marsh.

Full list

Best film in the international feature competition

Best film in the international short film competition

British animation
